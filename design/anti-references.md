# Anti-References — Don't Look Like These

## 1. Squishmallows — squishmallows.com
Screenshot: anti-references/squishmallows.png
What's generic: Loud, childish, toy-store energy. Bright primary colors, cartoon aesthetic, "collect them all" mentality. This is what happens when you design for kids instead of the adult buying for emotional wellness. Busy grids, no breathing room, no story.

## 2. Jellycat — jellycat.com
Screenshot: anti-references/jellycat.png
What's generic: Premium positioning but still "cute plush toy" framing. Product grids with white backgrounds, category-first navigation, no emotional narrative. Better than Squishmallows but still fundamentally a stuffed animal catalog, not a wellness brand.

## 3. Bearaby — bearaby.com
Screenshot: anti-references/bearaby.png
What's generic: Actually well-designed, but their specific patterns are now template-able. The stats-bar-below-hero, the 3-column benefit cards, the "designed for rest, backed by science" structure. This was novel 3 years ago. Now it's the default "premium comfort product" template. We need to go further.

## The Anti-Pattern Summary
All three sites treat their products as objects to be purchased. GiveHugz v4 should treat its products as companions to be met. The site should feel more like a wellness retreat than a store — closer to Rituals or a meditation app than any plush toy brand.
